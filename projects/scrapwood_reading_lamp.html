Scrapwood Reading Lamp

Let's make a Scrapwood Reading Lamp!

Let's make our own reading lamp using sawing, drilling, recycling, and assembly work. To do this, we can use materials like wood, an aluminum can, a lamp socket, screws, and bolts. Putting all this together we make a super-handy reading lamp!

Key STEM-skills

Constructing a reading lamp from scrapwood and recycled materials is a great all-round build project. Building reading lamps of all shapes and sizes are possible. The build project here is just one of many examples!

This STEM project helps students transition from simply following instructions to understanding the systems and mechanics behind the build:

* Engineering: building a functional reading lamp that stands upright and shines light where needed. Calculating how to balance the weight of the aluminum can and understanding why certain materials are chosen specifically.

* Circuitry & conductivity: learning the difference between a closed and open circuit and the importance of secure connections to maintain conductivity

* Tool proficiency (hard skills): this project builds 'manual literacy', familiarization with tools and autonomy, mechanical insight and measurement accuracy.

* Sustainability: waste and scrap as a resource, 'second life use' and the importance of upcycling and recycling materials.


Materials needed

Important:

The following list of materials is suited for a group of 12 participants. Based on practical experience, it is recommended providing spare materials to account for any potential mishaps or accidents.

  • This build uses European standards, including metric threads and units, for all parts and the 230V / 50hz electrical system.

  • For this build you can use a complete 'bulb fixture'. This includes the bulb socket, the power cable and the power switch already connected into one handy package.

    Building a reading lamp with this setup is certainly easier or fast since no electrical wiring is necessary. This however, makes the project more expensive compared to using cheaper, 'separate' electrical parts.

  • Using 'separate' electrical parts increases the complexity of this STEM-project but it also results in a more engaging learning experience. Budget-friendly electrical parts can be found at many online retailers.

    Here are some reference pictures for the parts needed, from left-to-right:

- E14 Threaded Backplate front & back
- E14 light socket and E14 bulb
- 'Type C' flat power plug ('Europlug') with inline switch

Step-by-step visual guide

Step 1: cutting wood to size and drilling holes

For this reading lamp you need the following wooden parts cut to size. You can use wood boards or reclaimed (scrap)wood.

  - Wooden board (base): 140 x 140 x 18mm - 1 piece
  - Wooden slat: 300 x 18 x 18mm - 1 piece
  - Wooden slat: 250 x 18 x 18mm - 1 piece
  - Wooden block: 70 x 45 x 18mm - 1 piece

After cutting your wooden parts to size, it's time to drill holes. For this, use an M8 drill bit and a power drill:

  • Wooden slat/arm (300 x 18 x 18mm), 300mm long: one hole 3.5 cm from the left edge, one hole 2.5 cm from the right edge.

  • Wooden slat/arm (250 x 18 x 18mm), 250mm long: one hole 3.5 cm from both the left and right edges.

  • Next drill a hole through the middle of the wooden block (70 x 45 x 18mm).

Step 2: attaching the wooden parts

Attach the longest wooden slat (300 x 18 x 18mm) to the shortest slat (250 x 18 x 18mm) using M8 bolts, washers and nuts. Tighten the bolts and nuts securely with an adjustable wrench.

  • Important: attach the shortest slat to the longest slat using the hole 2.5 cm from the right edge of the longest slat. Click on the picture below for reference.

Next, it's time to attach the wooden block (70 x 45 x 18mm) to the wooden base (140 x 140 x 18mm). Position the wood block carefully and use wood glue to attach it to the wooden base. Use a handy bar clamp to secure both wooden parts so the wood glue can dry nicely. Click on the picture below for a reference position.

Attach the wooden block to the base using 2 wood screws to provide extra stability and reinforcement. Drill two holes through the wooden base. Align the holes directly underneath the glued wooden block. Make sure you drill through the wooden base and about a 1cm through the wooden block ('pre-drilling'). Use a Philips screw driver to secure both parts with the wood screws.

The wooden structure of your reading lamp is now complete! Time to test some essential engineering aspects:

- Structural strenght: move the wood slats up & down
- Stability: Does the reading lamp sit upright when the wood slats are adjusted? Are the parts secured tightly using the bolts, nuts and washers?

Step 3: attaching the electrical parts to the tin can

In this step the E14 light socket and the power cord with inline switch are attached to the tin can. This also creates the electrical circuit of the reading lamp.

Make 2 holes into your tin can. The diameter of the holes should match the size of an M8 bolt. The first hole goes into the middle of the bottom of the can. Make a second hole on the side of the tin can, about 4cm from the bottom. Click on the picture below for a reference position.

Next, secure the light socket inside the tin can with an M10 threaded backplate. Ensure the backplate is fully tightened so that the socket is clamped firmly to the inside wall of the can.

Step 4: final assembly and testing

Attach the tin can to the shortest wooden slat (250 x 18 x 18mm) using an M8 bolt, an M8 nut, and two washers. Place one washer against the inside wall of the can and the other against the outside wall.

The reading lamp is now fully assembled. Time to plug it in and test it out!

* Not seeing the LED bulb light up? Don't panic, troubleshoot:
- are the electrical wires attached properly within the light socket?
- is something wrong with the inline switch on the power cord?
- is the light socket properly screwed together?
- is the 'center tab' of the light socket touching the LED bulb?
- is the LED bulb damaged or burnt out? Is it screwed in fully?
- troubleshoot!
