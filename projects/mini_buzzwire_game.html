Mini Buzz Wire game (no soldering)

Let's make a Mini Buzz Wire game!

Let's make our our own Mini buzz wire game! This is a true game of skill where you move a ring from one end of a metal wire to the other. But watch out: if the ring touches the wire, a light will turn on and you have to start over!

Key STEM-skills

* Engineering: an electrical circuit shows that electricity only flows when there is a continuous path from the power source (battery or USB-C), through the wire and ring, and back to the LED/Buzzer.

* Switch Mechanics: the wire buzzer game itself is actually an "open circuit or switch". Touching the wire closes the circuit, a core concept in digital electronics.

* Component Polarity: Using LED lights teaches that some components are directional (positive/negative), unlike a simple lightbulb.

* Conductivity: learning which materials allow electricity to pass (copper wire, metal ring) vs insulators (scrap wood, plastic tape).

* Tool Literacy: Using a power drill or Dremel builds fine motor skills and teaches safety protocols, such as securing the workpiece.

* Structural Integrity: using screws to mount the wire into the wood base and using wood glue teaches how to create a stable "chassis" for a project.

* Logic, troubleshooting and problem solving: The "Maker Mindset")

* Debugging: if the LED doesn't turn on when the wire is touched, the circuit, the parts or the "build" must be systematically checked for mistakes.

* Iteration: learning to "playtest" a design, 'Is the wire too easy?', 'Too hard?' and adjusting the difficulty of the game element by changing the wire's path.


Materials needed

Important:

The following list of materials is suited for a group of 12 participants. Based on practical experience, it is recommended providing spare materials to account for any potential mishaps or accidents.

  • For this build a 3D printed enclosure is used. A freely downloadable .stl file is provided which you can 3D print yourself. This is optional, there are of course alternatives to mount the electrical parts.

  • The use of a USB-C power socket is from a more ecological standpoint: this ensures that no batteries are needed to power the Buzz Wire Game.

  • The LED used in this project has a built-in ('prewired') resistor. Budget-friendly versions can be found at many online retailers. Here are some reference pictures of this type of LED:

Step-by-step visual guide

Step 1: drilling holes

Take your wooden base plate and make a pencil mark on the left and right side. Make sure the pencil marks are around 14cm apart. Now drill a hole with the diameter of your wood screws (4 x 30 in our case) through your pencil marks.

Next, drill a hole in the bottom of your wooden slats. Make sure to drill the hole exactly in the center. Important: do not drill too deep; a depth of 1cm is sufficient.

Step 2: attaching wooden parts

Place the bottom side of your wooden slats over the holes you drilled in your wooden board. Screw your wooden slats to the board using two wood screws and a Phillips head screwdriver.

Mark a pencil cross on the sides of your wooden slats. Do this at a height of approximately 4cm. Drill a hole through both pencil crosses and all the way through the wooden slats.

We're ready to attach our copper buzz wire!

Step 3: preparing copper wires

Take your 40cm long, Ø2.5mm copper wire and bend it into a fun and challenging shape. Important: Ensure that 4cm at both ends of the spiral wire remains perfectly straight!

Secure your copper wire firmly into the holes in your wooden slats. Bend the left side of your copper wire upwards to fix your spiral wire in place. Use long-nose pliers for this. Well done!

Take your 20cm long, Ø1.5mm copper wire and make a small loop at the end. Use your long-nose pliers again for this.

Step 4: preparing the electrical circuit

Attach a terminal block (wire connector) to the back end of your Ø1.5mm copper wire with the loop. Attach a second terminal block to the right side of your thick, Ø2.5mm copper wire. Use a flat-head screwdriver for this.

Take your 3D printed USB-C enclosure and turn it around. Now insert your USB-C power socket into the larger hole of the enclosure and push it in firmly. Next, insert your LED into the smaller hole of the enclosure and push it in firmly. If needed use some hot glue to secure both parts into the 3D enclosure.

Step 5: connecting the electrical wires

Important: the following step is very crucial for your buzzer wire game to work. Make sure you connect the correct wires to the correct parts!

- Connecting the black wires or '-' negative polarity:

Use your wire cutters to snip the black wire of your LED light. Make sure that you have approximately 2cm of black wire remaining. Use your wire cutters again to snip the black wire of your USB-C power socket. Make sure that you have approximately 2cm of black wire remaining.

Now use your wire stripper to strip about 1cm of both black wires. Next, twist both stripped cables together so that they do not come loose. Take a piece of electrical tape and wrap it firmly around the cables. Finally, make sure the black cable are firmly pushed into the 3D printed enclosure.

- Connecting the red wires or '+' positive polarity:

Take the red wire of your LED light, this has a '+' or 'positive' polarity, and attach it to the terminal block that is attached to your (thin) Ø1.5mm copper wire with the loop.

Next, take the red wire of your USB-C power socket, this has a '+' or 'positive' polarity, and connect it the terminal block that is attached to your (thick) Ø2.5mm copper wire.

Now would be a good time to test out your wiring and your electrical circuit: connect a USB-C to USB-A cable between the USB-C socket and a USB-PSU and touch the Ø2.5mm copper wire with your Ø1.5mm copper wire with the loop. If the LED lights up nicely, your electrical circuit works!

Step 6: installing the 3D enclosure

Glue your 3D printed enclosure to your right-hand wooden slat. Use fast-drying wood glue for this. Make sure to feed both red wires through the small square opening at the back of the 3D-printed casing.

Use a handy clamp to temporarily hold your enclosure to the wooden base plate. Wait about 10 minutes for the glue to dry.

Step 7: try-out out & troubleshooting

Connect a USB-C to USB-A cable between the USB-C socket and a USB charger and try out your mini buzz wire game!

* LED not lighting up? Don't panic, troubleshoot:
- are the electrical wires attached properly with the right polarity?
- are the electrical wires inserted properly into the terminal blocks?
- the twisted black wires: have they come loose? Or has the tape gotten loose?
- troubleshoot!
